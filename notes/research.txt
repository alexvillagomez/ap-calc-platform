# Adaptive Learning Research for AP Calculus Practice Platform

## Executive Summary

This document synthesizes research on optimal practice structures for adaptive math learning systems, with specific recommendations for the AP Calc platform. Key findings support a hybrid approach: introduce concepts through structured topic blocks with adaptive difficulty, incorporate interleaved practice for long-term retention and strategy flexibility, and apply spaced repetition with evidence-based review intervals.

---

## 1. Topic Structure: Blocks vs. Interleaving

### Research Findings

**Initial Learning (Blocked Practice)**
- Students learn mathematical content more quickly with blocked practice (all problems of one type together)
- Blocked practice feels more comfortable and achieves better immediate performance
- Students often misperceive blocked practice as more effective than it actually is

**Long-Term Retention & Transfer (Interleaved Practice)**
- Interleaved/mixed practice (problems of different types mixed together) produces significantly better long-term performance
- A landmark study of ~800 seventh-grade students in Florida showed:
  * Interleaved practice: 61% correct on delayed test (1 month later)
  * Blocked practice: 38% correct on the same test
  * 24-hour gap: interleaved (77%) vs. blocked (38%)

**Why Interleaving Works**
- Blocked practice lets students solve problems without choosing appropriate strategies—they know the strategy before reading the problem
- Interleaving forces students to decide *which* strategy applies, developing flexible and adaptive strategy use
- This cognitive effort is uncomfortable but essential for long-term mastery and transfer to new contexts

### Recommendation for AP Calc Platform

**Hybrid Structure:**
1. **Initial Mastery Phase (Blocked):** When a student first encounters a topic, present 3-5 focused problems of that single type to build fluency
2. **Consolidation Phase (Interleaved):** Once initial mastery is achieved, systematically interleave this topic with other related topics (e.g., derivatives mixed with integrals, limits, etc.)
3. **Spacing & Retrieval (Interleaved + Spaced):** In subsequent practice sessions, include this topic in mixed sets with increasing time gaps

This mirrors your hypothesis of "topic blocks" for introduction, but emphasizes the need to transition to interleaving for long-term retention.

**Evidence:** [Interleaving in Math](https://www.aft.org/ae/spring2020/agarwal_agostinelli); [Interleaved Learning in Elementary School Mathematics](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6385790/); [When to Block versus Interleave Practice](https://escholarship.org/content/qt4h12h31r/qt4h12h31r_noSplash_4e42bdca59a1dd71648e55d13e49781f.pdf?t=sgics3)

## 2. Spaced Repetition: Science & Optimal Intervals

### Core Principle

Spaced repetition leverages the "spacing effect"—material reviewed at strategically timed intervals is retained far longer than material crammed or blocked together. The optimal review timing is *just before* the student would forget, typically around 85-90% recall probability.

### Evidence-Based Review Schedules

**Standard Schedule (D = day of initial learning):**
- Review 1: D+1 (1 day later)
- Review 2: D+3 (3 days later)
- Review 3: D+7 (1 week)
- Review 4: D+14 (2 weeks)
- Review 5: D+30 (1 month)

Alternative schedule (also supported): days 1, 7, 16, 35 after initial learning.

**Key principle:** After just 5 spaced review sessions, information moves to long-term memory for most learners.

### Why Timing Matters

- **Review too early** → material not yet at forgetting threshold; weak consolidation effect
- **Review too late** → forced to relearn (inefficient time use)
- **85-90% recall window** → sweet spot for maximum retention with minimum effort

### Implementation Guidance

For AP Calculus:
- Students who master a topic should see a *new problem on that topic* approximately 1-3 days later
- If student struggles (< 70% success rate), maintain higher frequency (daily or every other day)
- If student demonstrates strong mastery (≥ 80% over 3+ attempts), space out to the full D+7, D+14, D+30 schedule
- Vary the difficulty and context (interleave with other topics) during spaced reviews

**Advantage:** Spaced practice improves long-term retention 200-300% compared to massed practice (repeated practice in one session).

**Evidence:** [Spaced Repetition Guide](https://revizly.app/blog/revision-espacee-guide); [Teacher's Guide to Spaced Repetition](https://thirdspacelearning.com/us/blog/spaced-repetition/); [Cognitive Science of Learning](https://www.justinmath.com/cognitive-science-of-learning-spaced-repetition/)

## 3. Advanced Spacing Algorithms: SM-2, FSRS, & IRT

### SM-2 Algorithm (Anki)

**What it is:** Mathematical formula developed by Piotr Wozniak (1987) for calculating optimal review intervals.

**How it works:**
- Assigns an "easiness factor" (EF) to each card/problem
- Adjusts EF based on performance: correct answers increase EF, wrong answers decrease it
- Calculates next review interval using: `Interval = Previous Interval × EF`

**Advantage:** Simpler to implement; proven effective (200-300% better retention than passive study).

**Limitation:** "Ease hell"—if a student struggles with a card, the EF drops permanently, causing intervals to become very short even after recovery. Difficult for students regaining momentum.

### FSRS Algorithm (Modern Standard, 2022)

**What it is:** Free Spaced Repetition Scheduler by Jarrett Ye; machine-learning-based refinement of SM-2.

**How it works:**
- Uses three variables: **Difficulty**, **Stability**, **Retrievability**
- Difficulty uses "mean reversion"—after correct answers, it gradually returns to baseline rather than staying permanently damaged
- Parameters are fitted to individual review history via machine learning

**Key advantage over SM-2:** Eliminates "ease hell"—recovering students get appropriate intervals sooner.

**Effectiveness:** Reduces required reviews by 20-30% for the same retention rate compared to SM-2.

**Adoption:** Now built into Anki (as of 2023), RemNote, and many educational apps. Proven in medical education, language learning, and STEM skill acquisition.

### Item Response Theory (IRT) Approach

**What it is:** Educational measurement framework that models ability and difficulty on a continuous scale.

**Implementation in adaptive systems:**
- Target 85% correct on problems at student's current skill level
- Increase difficulty when student consistently scores ≥ 85%
- Decrease difficulty when student falls below ~60%

**Advantage for AP Calc:** Provides continuous difficulty scaling rather than discrete levels; adapts to individual student trajectories.

### Recommendation for AP Calc Platform

**Progressive Implementation:**
1. **Phase 1 (MVP):** Simple rule-based spacing (D+1, D+3, D+7, D+14, D+30) triggered manually or by mastery thresholds
2. **Phase 2:** IRT-based difficulty targeting (aim for 70-85% success rate; adjust difficulty accordingly)
3. **Phase 3:** FSRS integration for optimal interval calculation per student per topic

Your current system in `apps/student/lib/practiceAlgorithm.ts` and `diagnosticScoring.ts` already implements EMA (exponential moving average) for skill tracking, which pairs well with FSRS.

**Evidence:** [Spaced Repetition Algorithms](https://www.mindomax.com/spaced-repetition-algorithms); [FSRS vs SM-2](https://www.neurako.com/blog/fsrs-vs-sm2-spaced-repetition-algorithms-compared); [SM-2 Algorithm Explained](https://tegaru.app/en/blog/sm2-algorithm-explained)

## 4. Difficulty Progression & Mastery Thresholds

### When to Increase Difficulty

**Rule-based thresholds:**
- **3 consecutive correct answers** → increase difficulty
- **1 incorrect answer** → return to previous difficulty level
- **Alternative:** Increase difficulty when success rate ≥ 80% over 5+ attempts; decrease when < 60%

**IRT approach (recommended):**
- Track student's estimated ability (skill level)
- Target difficulty where success probability = 70-85%
- Automatically adjust problem difficulty to maintain this zone

**Difficulty should increase gradually:**
- Avoid jumps that exceed student's ZPD (zone of proximal development)
- Each difficulty step should feel achievable with effort, not overwhelming

### Mastery Thresholds

**Standard thresholds (from research):**
- **80% correct** over 5-10 problems → foundational mastery
- **90% correct** over 9-10 problems → strong mastery (correlates with better long-term retention)
- **4/5 correct** → acceptable for complex, time-consuming problems (like multi-step calculus problems)

**Effect of threshold level:**
- 90% threshold produces higher long-term retention than 80%
- 80% threshold produces higher retention than 50%
- Higher thresholds correlate with better response maintenance over time

**For AP Calculus specifics:**
- Simpler topics (e.g., derivative power rule): 90% over 10 problems
- Complex topics (e.g., u-substitution, curve sketching): 80% over 5-8 problems
- Multi-step problems: 4/5 correct or 80% is reasonable

### Recommended Progression

```
Difficulty 1 (Computational): Isolated, straightforward application
  Mastery: 90% correct over 10 problems
  Next: Move to Difficulty 2

Difficulty 2 (Multi-step): Requires multiple concepts, but still guided
  Mastery: 80% correct over 8 problems
  Next: Move to Difficulty 3

Difficulty 3 (Conceptual): Requires strategy selection; less guidance
  Mastery: 75% correct over 6+ problems
  Next: Spaced review in mixed context; don't increase further

Difficulty 4 (Challenge/Synthesis): Novel contexts; high cognitive load
  Target: Present after Difficulty 3 mastery + 2-week spacing
  Purpose: Deep transfer, not frequent practice
```

**Evidence:** [Demonstrating and Assessing Mastery](https://www.gettingsmart.com/2019/09/11/demonstrating-and-assessing-mastery-and-managing-mastery-learning-data/); [Mastery Thresholds Examples](https://www.gettingsmart.com/2019/09/18/examples-of-mastery-thresholds-to-enable-mastery-learning-in-multiple-subjects/); [Challenge of Challenges in Mastery Learning](https://educationaldatamining.org/edm2024/proceedings/2024.EDM-doctoral-consortium.124/index.html)

## 5. Struggling vs. Advancing Students

### Struggling Students (< 60% success rate)

**Intervention principles:**
- **Decrease difficulty immediately** (return to Difficulty 1 or 2)
- **Increase practice frequency** (daily or every other day, not spaced)
- **Maintain blocking** (same topic for 5-10 problems before interleaving)
- **Add scaffolding:** Hints, worked examples, conceptual foundation checks
- **Shorter problem sets** to prevent frustration; more frequent success experiences

**Specific actions:**
1. Check for prerequisite gaps (e.g., if struggling with derivatives, diagnose calculus prerequisites)
2. Provide 1-2 worked examples of the exact problem type
3. Reduce problem difficulty by 1-2 levels and rebuild
4. Increase success target from 70% to just 50-60% initially; rebuild confidence
5. Transition back to spaced repetition only after ≥ 75% success over 5+ attempts

**Perception note:** Students often feel that struggling is ineffective, but it's actually a sign of productive learning if difficulty is calibrated correctly. Educators should frame difficulty as "stretch" not "failure."

### Advancing Students (≥ 80% success rate)

**Acceleration principles:**
- **Increase difficulty more aggressively** (move from Difficulty 1 → 3 after 6-8 correct)
- **Introduce interleaving sooner** (after 5-6 blocked problems instead of 10)
- **Sparse out review intervals** (D+7, D+21, D+60 instead of D+1, D+3, D+7)
- **Offer challenge problems** (Difficulty 4) with novel contexts or multiple concepts
- **Mix topics earlier** in their learning trajectory

**Specific actions:**
1. Diagnostic pretest to identify already-mastered topics
2. Skip Difficulty 1-2 for advanced students; start at Difficulty 2-3
3. Offer "bonus" or "extension" problems (multi-concept synthesis) after mastery
4. Transition to true spaced repetition with long intervals (14-30 days)
5. Encourage self-explanation and teaching peers

**Perception benefit:** Advanced students perceive challenge (reasonable difficulty) as more effective than easy practice; maintain their motivation through progressive difficulty.

### Balancing Within Mixed Cohorts

**For teachers/platforms with mixed-ability students:**
- Use adaptive algorithms to create personalized paths (not one-size-fits-all)
- Allow Difficulty 1/2 students and Difficulty 4 students to work on the same topic in parallel
- Use interleaving strategically: Difficulty 1 students benefit from blocked + some interleaving; advanced students need more interleaving
- Track "attempt count" per topic—don't force advanced students through 10 problems if 5 suffice

**Evidence:** [Math Strategies for Struggling Students](https://blog.acceleratelearning.com/math-strategies-for-struggling-students); [Mathematics Interventions](https://educationnorthwest.org/resources/mathematics-interventions-what-strategies-work-for-struggling-students); [Students' Perceptions of Effective Math Learning Strategies](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC12383065/)

## 6. Problems Per Topic Before Moving On

### Research-Based Guidance

**Minimum exposure:**
- 5-6 problems of the same type for initial fluency (blocked practice)
- Then transition to interleaving/spacing

**Standard full mastery:**
- 8-10 problems for simple, computational topics
- 5-8 problems for complex, conceptual topics (e.g., curve sketching, optimization)
- 4-5 problems for very time-consuming topics (e.g., multi-step application problems)

**Variation by complexity:**
| Topic Type | Problem Count | Mastery Threshold |
|---|---|---|
| Simple computation (power rule, basic limits) | 10 | 90% |
| Multi-step procedures (u-substitution, integration by parts) | 8 | 80% |
| Conceptual & strategic (when to use which method) | 6 | 75% |
| Synthesis/challenge (novel contexts) | 3-4 | 70% |

### For AP Calculus Specifically

- **Foundational topics** (limits, continuity, derivatives): 10 problems at Difficulty 1
- **Procedural topics** (differentiation rules, antiderivatives): 8 problems mixed Difficulties 1-2
- **Application topics** (optimization, related rates): 6-8 problems mixed Difficulties 2-3
- **Advanced synthesis** (full curve sketches, multi-concept problems): 3-5 problems at Difficulties 3-4, highly spaced

### Moving On (When to Progress)

**Decision point:**
- Attempt count ≥ min threshold (5-10 depending on complexity)
- Success rate ≥ mastery threshold (75-90%)
- 3+ consecutive correct (optional additional gate for high-stakes topics)

**Do NOT wait for:**
- "Perfect" performance (85% is sufficient to progress and interleave)
- Arbitrary time limits
- All students ready (use adaptive routing)

**Implement early interleaving:**
Once 5-6 initial problems are solved correctly, introduce 1 problem from a *related* topic (e.g., if practicing derivatives, include 1 limit or integration problem per 4 derivative problems). This accelerates the learning-how-to-choose-strategy phase.

**Evidence:** [Mastery Learning Data](https://www.gettingsmart.com/2019/09/11/demonstrating-and-assessing-mastery-and-managing-mastery-learning-data/); [Does Practice Make Perfect?](https://educationaldatamining.org/edm2022/proceedings/2022.EDM-short-papers.27/index.html)

## 7. Adaptive Learning: Putting It All Together

### Recommended System Architecture

**Phase 1: Diagnostic & Topic Selection**
- Administer diagnostic quiz (5-7 questions) covering foundational prerequisites
- Score using EMA (exponential moving average)
- Route to topic based on gaps identified

**Phase 2: Introduction & Blocked Practice**
- Introduce topic with 1 worked example + explanation
- Present 5-8 blocked problems (same type) at Difficulty 1
- Target: 80%+ success
- If struggling: provide hint, show solution, practice again
- If succeeding: move to Phase 3

**Phase 3: Consolidation & Early Interleaving**
- Mix in related topics (e.g., derivatives + limits + continuity)
- Present 3-5 more problems at Difficulties 1-2, interleaved
- Apply EMA scoring to track mastery

**Phase 4: Spaced Review**
- Schedule topic problems at D+1, D+3, D+7, D+14 intervals
- Use interleaved, increased-difficulty versions
- Adjust spacing based on success:
  * ≥ 75% correct → lengthen spacing
  * < 60% correct → shorten spacing, add hint
- Update EMA skill scores per attempt

**Phase 5: Advancement & Challenge**
- Once ≥ 80% over 6+ spaced attempts, offer Difficulty 3-4 problems
- Include in mixed practice sets (once weekly or biweekly)
- Track "advanced mastery" separately from foundational mastery

### Key Metrics to Track

1. **Per-student:**
   - EMA skill score per topic (range 0-1)
   - Attempt count per topic
   - Success rate (% correct) per difficulty level
   - Last review date + next scheduled review date

2. **Per-problem:**
   - Attempt count
   - Success rate
   - Estimated difficulty (EMA-based)
   - Word difficulty (how often chosen vs. total pool)

3. **System-wide:**
   - Average topic mastery time to 80% success
   - Retention rate (% of students who maintain 70%+ score after 30-day gap)
   - Interleaving effect (do students in mixed practice sets score better on transfer tasks?)

### Student Experience Flow (from CLAUDE.md alignment)

Your current `/learn` state machine in `apps/student/app/` is well-aligned:

```
diagnostic → results → lesson/refresher/targeted → practice → spaced_review
```

**Recommended enhancement:**
- **Diagnostic phase:** Use 5-7 MCQs on umbrella topics; classify into 3 tiers (needs foundation lesson, needs refresher, ready for practice)
- **Lesson/Refresher phase:** Use your existing on-demand generation; add interleaved problems toward the end
- **Practice phase:** Implement IRT-based difficulty targeting (your `scoreProblem()` function already does this)
- **Spaced review:** Track next-review-date; inject D+1, D+7, D+21 problems into the practice queue at the scheduled times

Your `learn_student_keyword_states` table with `spaced_review_due_at` is the right structure; implement the scheduling logic in `apps/student/app/api/learn/practice/next`.

**Evidence:** [Adaptive Learning in Mathematics](https://www.sowiso.com/blog/adaptive-learning-mathematics/); [How Mastery Learning Algorithms Support Adaptive Math Learning](https://www.playpowerlabs.com/post/how-mastery-learning-algorithms-can-support-adaptive-math-learning-in-the-classroom); [Current Practices in Artificial Intelligence in Education](https://files.eric.ed.gov/fulltext/EJ1469663.pdf)

## 8. Key Takeaways for AP Calc Platform

### Your Hypothesis: Validated & Refined

Your intuition to "introduce topics in blocks, feed more questions when struggling, sparse out once mastered, return later" is **evidence-based and sound**. Refine it as follows:

1. **Introduction (Days 1-2):** 5-8 blocked problems, Difficulty 1, target 80%+
2. **Early interleaving (Days 2-3):** Mix in related topics; 3-5 more problems, Difficulties 1-2
3. **Consolidation (Days 4-7):** Spaced reviews at D+1, D+3; increase to Difficulty 2-3
4. **Long-term spacing (Weeks 2-4):** D+7, D+14, D+30 reviews; interleaved with other topics
5. **Mastery confirmation:** Once ≥ 80% over 6+ spaced attempts, topic moves to "mastered" state
6. **Challenge phase:** Offer Difficulty 4 problems with novel contexts, 1-2x per week

### Critical Implementation Points

1. **Don't force interleaving too early** → Students need fluency before strategy flexibility
2. **Don't delay interleaving too long** → Interleaved practice is essential for long-term transfer
3. **Balance challenge with success** → Target 70-85% success; boredom < 70%, frustration > 90%
4. **Track spaced reviews explicitly** → Use a `next_review_date` column; don't rely on student intuition
5. **Struggling students need scaffolding** → Reduce difficulty, increase hints, lengthen intervals between hard problems
6. **Advanced students need challenge** → Increase difficulty, introduce interleaving sooner, offer synthesis problems

### Next Steps

1. **Immediate (this sprint):**
   - Implement mastery threshold gates: 80% over 6+ attempts → mark topic "mastered"
   - Add `next_review_date` logic to `learn_student_keyword_states`
   - Schedule D+1, D+3, D+7 reviews in `practice/next` route

2. **Medium-term (1-2 sprints):**
   - Implement IRT-based difficulty targeting in `scoreProblem()` (target 75-80% success)
   - Add interleaving logic: after 6 blocked problems, start mixing 1-in-4 related topics
   - Track attempt count per topic; measure time-to-mastery

3. **Long-term (future roadmap):**
   - Integrate FSRS algorithm for optimal spacing per student
   - Implement challenge problem logic (Difficulty 4) for post-mastery review
   - A/B test blocked vs. interleaved; measure retention and transfer on AP exam
