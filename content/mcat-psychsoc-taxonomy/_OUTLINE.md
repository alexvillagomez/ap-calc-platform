## 6A · Sensing the Environment
Focus: how psychological, sociocultural, and biological factors shape how we sense and perceive the world; sensation → perception across the senses.

- **Sensory Processing (PSY, BIO)**
  - Sensation
    - Threshold
    - Weber's Law (PSY)
    - Signal detection theory (PSY)
    - Sensory adaptation
    - Psychophysics
  - Sensory receptors
    - Sensory pathways
    - Types of sensory receptors
- **Vision (PSY, BIO)**
  - Structure and function of the eye
  - Visual processing
    - Visual pathways in the brain
    - Parallel processing (PSY)
    - Feature detection (PSY)
- **Hearing (PSY, BIO)**
  - Structure and function of the ear
  - Auditory processing (e.g., auditory pathways in the brain)
  - Sensory reception by hair cells
- **Other Senses (PSY, BIO)**
  - Somatosensation (e.g., pain perception)
  - Taste (e.g., taste buds (chemoreceptors) that detect specific chemicals)
  - Smell
    - Olfactory cells (chemoreceptors) that detect specific chemicals
    - Pheromones (BIO)
    - Olfactory pathways in the brain (BIO)
  - Kinesthetic sense (PSY)
  - Vestibular sense
- **Perception (PSY)**
  - Bottom-up/top-down processing
  - Perceptual organization (e.g., depth, form, motion, constancy)
  - Gestalt principles

> **BOUNDARY (critical):** The **Biology** section already owns ALL sensory-organ biology and receptor/transduction biology in its "Sensory Systems and Transduction" umbrella, with these existing keywords: *Sensory receptor types and transduction; Eye structure and function; Photoreceptors (rods/cones); Ear structure and function; Auditory transduction by hair cells; Vestibular sense and balance; Taste as chemoreception; Smell as chemoreception; Somatosensation and pain; Proprioception and kinesthesis.* **Do NOT recreate any of those anatomy/receptor/transduction-biology keywords.** Your job is the **PSYCHOLOGY of sensation & perception:** psychophysics (absolute & difference thresholds, Weber's law, signal detection theory + its hits/misses/false-alarms framing, sensory adaptation, subliminal stimuli), the perceptual "processing" aspects (bottom-up vs top-down, parallel processing, feature detection, visual/auditory pathway *processing* as cognition), perceptual organization (depth, form, motion, constancy — including monocular/binocular depth cues), and Gestalt principles (each principle can be its own keyword: figure-ground, proximity, similarity, closure, continuity, common fate). Frame any sense's content as **perception**, not anatomy.
